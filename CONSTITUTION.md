## Purpose

VMS exists to build a trusted digital ecosystem for mapping, surveying, land valuation, and property information.

The platform is designed for professionals and the public alike, making reliable geographic and property information accessible to everyone.

---

## Core Principles

### 1. Public First

Maps, property records, government rates, and public information should remain accessible to everyone.

Knowledge should never be locked behind unnecessary barriers.

---

### 2. Professionals Empower Professionals

Surveyors, valuers, engineers, architects, government officials, and other professionals should have tools that improve their daily work without unnecessary complexity.

---

### 3. Simplicity Before Features

Every feature must solve a real problem.

Features that add complexity without meaningful value should not be included.

---

### 4. Accuracy Over Appearance

Beautiful interfaces are important.

Reliable information is essential.

Whenever there is a conflict, accuracy always comes first.

---

### 5. Community Contribution

VMS should encourage responsible contributions from users while maintaining trusted and verifiable information.

---

### 6. Sustainable Development

Core public features should remain available to everyone.

Professional tools may become premium only after the platform reaches sustainable adoption.

---

### 7. Privacy and Security

User data belongs to the user.

Authentication, personal information, and records must always be handled responsibly.

---

### 8. Build for the Future

Every decision should still make sense five years from now.

Temporary trends should never replace long-term thinking.

---

### 9. Universal Access

Information that benefits society—such as maps, public property records, government rates, and public contacts—should remain openly accessible.

Professional productivity tools may require a subscription in the future, but public information should remain available to everyone.

---

## Development Philosophy

Before implementing any feature, ask:

1. What problem are we solving?
2. Who benefits from this?
3. Is this the simplest possible solution?
4. Can we build it using our current tools?
5. Will we still be proud of this decision five years from now?

If the answer is "No," reconsider the feature.
